# HOMILY

The Temptations of Jesus come immediately after Jesus's baptism.

In Matthew's version, Jesus is led by the Spirit Into the wilderness. 

I think this is something which we're going to get repeated later in Lent: immediately after a revelation of Jesus's sonship that 'this is my son in whom I am well pleased', there comes a 'down' moment for Jesus when he has to live through the consequences of this.

We'll have that at the Transfiguration also - the next big 'this is my son' moment, for then he goes down and lives out what that means in Jerusalem.

Here Matthew gives us a picture of what Jesus's baptism meant. 

Particularly one element of it I brought out when we looked at Jesus baptism: the different sorts of water that he was fulfilling, but one was coming out of the waters of the Red Sea, the extent to which Jesus was the fulfillment of the Mosaic promise.

This is what we're going to get today's Gospel.

A good deal of the Jesus's Temptations here is Jesus fulfilling Israel, showing what Israel was really all about and not succumbing to the Temptations that Israel had succumbed to.

```
Then Jesus was led up by the Spirit into the wilderness to be tempted by the devil.
```

'Led up' this is an a place of upwards movement. 

It's not cast out into the wilderness, it slid up into the wilderness to be tempted by the devil, here simply referred to as the Greek word that diabolos.

```
He fasted for forty days and forty nights, and afterwards he was famished.
```

Now this refers to the people of Israel's 40 years in the desert before getting to the Holy Land. 

More importantly, it refers to Moses and to Elijah, both of whom fasted for 40 days and 40 nights before particularly important revelations.

On the case of Moses, it was before he gave the Ten Commandments.

On the case of Elijah, it was before he received his next prophetic mission.

So this is setting us up for the next time when Moses and Elijah come together with Jesus for Transfiguration.

So Jesus fasted 40 days and 40 nights and afterwards he was famished.

I don't actually know why they say he was famished - I imagine he was, but the actual Greek just said he was hungry. There's no particular emphasis on it though.

```
The tempter came and said to him ...
```

I like the notion of the beguiler - the one who beguiles -, because there's interesting suggestion: 

It's only in the process of undergoing the Temptation that Jesus recognizes who the tempter is, because he only refers to him as Satan at the end.

```
... if you are the Son of God, command these stones to become loaves of bread.
```

Now, the key is in the word 'if'. The beguiling is in the 'if'.

Jesus has just heard the voice, which has said to him and to those listening 'this is my son in whom I am well pleased'. In other words, he knows his identity. 

The point of the devil is to suggest that you might be feeling a little bit unsure about it and so needs to do something to demonstrate it.

So the first great attempt to undermine Jesus's knowledge of who he is and to get him to do what so often we do, which is to be triggered into doing something that will make us feel that we're doing something right rather than knowing and allowing ourselves to relax into being Sons and Daughters of God.

```
But he answered, ‘It is written,
“One does not live by bread alone, but by every word that comes from the mouth of God.”’
```

He's quoting from the book of Deuteronomy, which remember is Moses's sermon to the people of Israel concerning what they screwed up and what they must now get right.

This refers to the passage of Deuteronomy that looks back at Exodus and the time of manna in the desert.

God gave manna and the question was: you must learn to trust not to take more than you need, because I will give you what you need.

So Jesus sees the Temptation, which is that of Exodus, of being able to have something more just in case.

Jesus will eventually become the bread whom he gives.

He will eventually become the bread and give himself as bread to us, but that's through becoming what he really is and knows: the Son of God.

```
Then the devil took him to the holy city and placed him on the pinnacle of the temple, 6 saying to him, ‘If you are the Son of God, throw yourself down; for it is written,

“He will command his angels concerning you”, and “On their hands they will bear you up, so that you will not dash your foot against a stone.”’
```

This is a quote from Psalm 91.

Well, if in the previous Temptation, it was Jesus, it was Israel and its prophetic life, the Moses Exodus life, here it is the Priestly life or Pinnacle of the temple: someone on the top of it throwing himself down.

The purpose of the Priestly sacrifice in the temple was that it was God coming down and giving himself up as a sacrifice for humans, so that they could be covered by his blood, for which the lamb was understanding.

That would have been the ancient right of the atonement and Satan, in one sense, is saying to Jesus: 'You're going to be able to do this sacrifice, but preempted. Just go ahead, do it.'

So the Priestly temptation of thinking is that the temple itself will be a base of security.

```
Jesus said to him, ‘Again it is written, “Do not put the Lord your God to the test.”’
```

That likewise is a quote from Deuteronomy. 

In this case, it refers to the occasion of manna in the in the desert when the people complained and wanted water and they put God to the test.

So Jesus responds to them again with Deuteronomy, again showing that this is part of what's absolutely central to Israel's faith: carrying on the journey, not putting people, not putting God to the test.

Note so far that we've had a reference in the first place to bread and the second place to water. 

The third reference is to power.

```
Again, the devil took him to a very high mountain and showed him all the kingdoms of the world and their splendour; 9 and he said to him, ‘All these I will give you, if you will fall down and worship me.’
```

In other words, all of these are within my power, all you need to do is sign up to me and you will become the ruler of them just as I am.

At this stage, Jesus recognizes him. 

He's no longer beguiled, no longer just a conceivable chance you might be right so that I need to hear the word.

```
Jesus said to him, ‘Away with you, Satan! for it is written,

“Worship the Lord your God, and serve only him.”’
```

Likewise a passage from Deuteronomy, very close actually to the previous passage from Deuteronomy and it appears to refer to idolatry.

So you have the bread the water and idolatry.

Idolatry of the calf, but all idolatry encompass ultimately to a question of power. 

Jesus knows perfectly well that, if Satan has the power, it is because people give him the power and think they're getting it if you go along with the lie. 

You think you're becoming triumphant through the light, but in fact the lie is running you. 

That's how the power of association works: convincing you that you're being given powers. 

In fact, you're being sucked into a bar that you don't in fact run, but which turns you inside out.

Now please notice what Jesus has done. 

He has rejected the three classic messianic temptations: that of prophet, that of priest and that of King.

He is going to redefine hereafter what is meant by prophet, what is meant by priest and what is meant by King.

The principle of power, the principle of blessing and the principle of speaking and living the truth.

These three are going to be radically altered by what Jesus is going to live through hereafter. 

```
Then the devil left him, and suddenly angels came and waited on him.
```

So it's interesting that the movement from the water of baptism, which in Luke he comes straight up and the senses that already his ordination has kicked in; and so now he is able to be with in the Holy Place.

The Holy Place has now become associated with him. 

Here it's interesting that the wilderness acts as a psychological step through into the Holy Place, so that the real one is not simply something that happens by an extrinsic act like baptism, but it's something that actually we grow through and into by our baptism like by Jesus's baptism as we become the priest, the prophet and the King. 

So we are to learn to say no to the shortcuts, to agree to be held in being and allow ourselves to be given the capacity to be good to others, to be given the capacity to forgive and make forgiveness available for others and to be given the capacity to exercise the kind of power that actually helps people live in truth. 

Of these are processes into which we are taken by baptism and Jesus is showing us that this is the true path of Israel.
